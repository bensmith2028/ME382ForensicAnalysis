# ME382ForensicAnalysis
Final project for ME382: Experiments in Micro and Nano Science and Engineering taken in Spring 2026. Reverse engineering of tempered glass screen protector coating methods using Atomic Force Microscopy (AFM) in an NUFAB cleanroom.

Usage Instructions: Install all files, run the .m and you'll see pretty graphs.

Abstract:
Screen protectors are a ubiquitous consumer product, yet the surface finishing processes used in their manufacture are rarely characterized at the nanoscale. Understanding these processes is relevant not only to product quality — affecting optical clarity, scratch resistance, and glare — but also to the broader question of whether surface metrology tools like Atomic Force Microscopy (AFM) can serve as forensic instruments for reverse-engineering manufacturing methods from finished surfaces.

Glass surfaces are processed through two primary finishing methods. Grinding uses fixed or loose hard abrasives to mechanically cut between a rigid carrier and the glass surface, producing a surface roughness in the range of 0.1–1.0 µm. This is typically followed by polishing, which uses abrasives softer than the glass to remove only minute amounts of material, reducing roughness to the 0.5–2.0 nm range.1 For standard non-anti-reflective tempered glass, we therefore hypothesize that the surface undergoes a sequential grinding → polishing process. Anti-glare (AG) glass, by contrast, requires a rougher, diffusely scattering surface and is produced through one of three distinct methods: chemical etching, which transforms the surface into a matte texture; haze coating with micro-textured polarizer treatment; or sputter coating applied to the glass surface. Because each of these processes produces a characteristic surface texture at a different length scale, we hypothesized that AFM topography data could be used to distinguish between them.

To test both hypotheses, we acquired two commercially available tempered glass screen protectors — one standard and one anti-glare — and analyzed them using AFM. The AFM operates by moving a nanoscale cantilever tip across a substrate and measuring tip deflection as a function of position. This deflection is converted into quantitative surface maps of height and friction. We used tapping mode for topographic imaging, which minimizes tip-sample contact forces and is well suited to measuring nanometer-scale roughness without damaging the surface, and contact/lateral mode for friction analysis. Because grinding and polishing produce roughness signatures spanning the µm to sub-nm range, and because etching and coating each produce distinct surface textures, AFM resolution — on the order of nanometers — makes it uniquely appropriate for distinguishing between these processes forensically.

The goal of this study was therefore twofold: to characterize the surface finish of each screen protector and infer its likely manufacturing process, and to evaluate AFM as a practical tool for forensic surface analysis of consumer glass products.
